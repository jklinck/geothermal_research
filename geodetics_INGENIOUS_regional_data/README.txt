Geotiffs and accompanying ASCII, .csv, and metadata that describe geodetic shear and dilation models for the INGENIOUS study area, developed at the Nevada Geodetic Laboratory at the Nevada Bureau of Mines and Geology (NBMG).

Rate units are 10E-9/yr.

ASCI and .csv contain points in WGS84. Geotiffs have been projected and resampled to a custom INGENIOUS grid in NAD83 Contiguous USA Albers, with central meridian of -117.

Model details (full documentation on model methodology is included in the archive):

We use a combination of the robust MELD algorithm (Kreemer et al., 2018, 2020) and the methodology of ‘Haines and Holt’ (Haines and Holt, 1993; Holt et al., 2000; Kreemer et al., 2000; Beavan and Haines, 2001). We first take the geodetic velocity field and use it as input to the MELD algorithm to estimate a robust, but smooth, strain rate field. We use a minimum number of 56 triangles per grid point and set σmax to 35. σmax puts an upper limit on the allowed strain rate uncertainty, which is controlled by the size/shape of the triangle, and our chosen value ensures a minimum triangle size corresponding to a circle radius of 15 km. After running MELD, some velocities end up having not been used (because they are always causing outlier strain rates), and we subsequently remove those. Additionally, we remove any station for which the weighted residual velocity is larger than 1.2, or the weighted residual velocity is larger than 0.25 while the absolute residual velocity is larger than 5 mm/yr. These thresholds were chosen such that the outlier velocity detection was reasonable. The reduced velocity data base is then put through MELD one more time.

For our final geodetic strain rate model, we use the Haines and Holt method. This technique uses spline functions to fit a continuous velocity gradient tensor field to the data (in this case GPS velocities, but in next section geologic strain rate ‘data’). We create a grid with 0.2° by 0.15° cells (in east and north dimension, respectively). We only define the offshore area north of the Mendocino Triple Junction (i.e., the Juan de Fuca plate) to be rigid, and this area acts as the reference frame for the modeling. We do not make the western edge of the grid south of the Mendocino Triple Junction (i.e., the Pacific plate) rigid, because we wish to minimize creating any artificial strain rates between the western most stations offshore California and the edge of the grid. The eastern edge of the grid is also free to deform; we do not constrain it to be rigid (i.e., North America plate), because of the shown internal deformation of the North American plate (Kreemer et al., 2018). 

The Haines and Holt technique is a Bayesian type approach in which an a priori strain rate covariance matrix needs to be supplied. We choose an empirical approach in which we use the strain rate tensor calculated from the second iteration MELD model for the midpoints used for the Haines and Holt grid. We do this by calculating the second invariant of the MELD strain rates and assign that value to the standard deviation in ε ˙_xx and ε ˙_yy and the second invariant divided by √2 for the standard deviation in ε ˙_xy. We introduce a scaling factor with which those a priori values are being multiplied in order to end up with a median weighted residual velocity from the Haines and Holt model that is approximately 2.0. This is obtained when that scaling factor is 0.175. We choose the median misfit because we will inevitably have large misfits across the creeping portion of the SAFS and we do not want those to affect the overall fit between modeled and observed velocities. The weighted RMS between GPS velocities and model velocities at the GPS locations is 4.9. 

We infer from the geodetic strain rate model the principal strain rates, ε ˙_1 and ε ˙_2, and calculate the second invariant (√(ε ˙_1^2+ε ˙_2^2 )), the dilatation rate (ε ˙_1+ε ˙_2), and shear rate (min(|ε ˙_1 |,|ε ˙_2 |) when ε ˙_1 and ε ˙_2 have opposite signs, otherwise zero).
